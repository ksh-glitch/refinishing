The Workshop Journal · Shipping an heirloom

Shipping an heirloom to the workshop.

Most of the furniture we restore rides in our own van — pick-up and delivery are available, quoted with your estimate across Washington DC, Maryland and Virginia. But some pieces come farther. Over the years, clients across the country have shipped furniture to the bench. This is our honest guide to that decision: when shipping makes sense, how the estimate works at a distance, and what you — not we — will need to arrange.

01 — The DecisionWhen shipping makes sense

Before anything is wrapped

Most furniture shouldn’t travel.

We will say the unhelpful part first: for most pieces, a good workshop near you is the right answer. Freight in both directions costs real money and puts the piece in a second set of hands. That math has to be honest before a single blanket goes around a leg.

Shipping earns its keep in a narrower case. The piece is rare, or it is irreplaceable to your family — the chest that crossed an ocean once already, the desk with a grandmother’s initials inside the drawer. The work it needs is particular. And there is no shop within reach you would trust with it.

When those things line up, distance stops mattering. Wood is wood at the bench, whether it arrived from three miles away or three thousand. The photographs, the quote, the stripping by hand, the finish — none of it changes.

Start with the photos, not the freight. Before you price a single carrier, send us pictures. If the piece doesn’t justify the trip, we’ll say so, and it costs you nothing to hear it.

* Whether a piece deserves restoring at all is its own question, with its own guide — Is it worth restoring?

02 — The EstimatePhotos first

Same as local, at any distance

The quote travels by photograph.

  1. Photograph the piece

    The whole piece from a few steps back, then the damage up close — lifting veneer, loose joints, the failed finish. Daylight helps. Open a drawer if the maker left a mark inside; it tells us things.

  2. Send photos and a short description

    Use the free estimate form or email estimates@refinishing.org. Tell us where the piece lives, what happened to it, and what you want it to be when it comes home.

  3. We reply with an honest quote

    The same quote a neighbor in the DMV gets. Free, no obligation. This is where we tell you plainly whether the work justifies shipping — or whether a shop closer to you is the better call.

  4. You arrange the carrier

    If the numbers make sense, you book an insured furniture carrier to bring the piece to the workshop, and another — or the same one — to bring it home when the work is done. The next section covers how to choose one.

03 — The FreightWhat you arrange

We restore. We don’t ship.

The carrier is yours.

Let’s be plain about the division of labor: we do not crate furniture and we do not arrange freight. The shipping, in both directions, is yours — a carrier you choose, on your contract, insured in your name, so the piece stays under your protection from your door to ours and back. Furniture movers who work “blanket-wrap” carry un-crated pieces padded and wrapped in the truck; that trade exists for exactly this job. Interview a carrier the way you’d interview us. These are the questions worth asking.

“What is the piece insured for, and by whom?” Get the coverage in writing before pickup. Ask the difference between the carrier’s standard valuation — often set per pound — and full-value insurance on the piece itself. For an heirloom, per-pound valuation is close to no insurance at all.

Ask first · In writing

“Blanket-wrap or boxed — and who wraps it?” Ask whether the crew pads and wraps the piece in your home or expects it packaged at the curb. Ask what they wrap with, and how a carved leg or a mirror gets protected.

The wrapping question

“Does it change trucks?” Ask whether the same crew and vehicle carry the piece door to door, or whether it passes through terminals and transfers along the way. Every extra loading is an extra chance to drop something that survived a century.

Handling & transfers

“When does it arrive, and who do I call?” Ask for the pickup window, the delivery window, and a person — not a portal — you can reach while the piece is on the road. Then tell us the schedule, so we’re ready at the door when it arrives.

04 — The AlternativeCloser to home

The part that costs us work

Sometimes the answer is a shop near you.

If you live in Washington DC, Maryland or Virginia, don’t ship anything — we pick up and deliver — quoted with your estimate — and we’d rather carry your piece ourselves.

If you live farther away, weigh the local option honestly, because we will. A capable restorer in your own town spares you two freight bills and two truck rides. Vet them the way you’d vet us: ask whether they strip by hand rather than dipping, ask to see before-and-after work, ask how they quote and whether they want money up front. Good answers to those questions matter more than our sign on the door.

And if you send us photographs and the truthful reply is “this doesn’t need us — it needs someone within driving distance of you,” that is the reply you’ll get. A workshop that has been paid on trust since 1991 stays in business by giving that answer whenever it’s the true one.

05 — QuestionsAsked from far away

Answered honestly

What distant clients ask.

Do you crate the piece or arrange the freight?

No — we are restorers, not shippers. You arrange the carrier in both directions, chosen and insured by you, so the piece stays under your protection door to door. Insured blanket-wrap furniture carriers handle un-crated furniture as a matter of course; ask them about insurance in writing, who wraps the piece and where, and whether it changes trucks along the way. While the piece is in the workshop, it is under our roof and in our care.

How does the estimate work if I’m not local?

Exactly the way it works for a neighbor in Washington DC, Maryland or Virginia. Send a few photographs and a short description through our free estimate form or to estimates@refinishing.org — the whole piece, the damage up close, taken in daylight. We reply with an honest quote. The estimate is free and there is no obligation, wherever you live.

Is it worth shipping furniture across the country to be restored?

Usually not — and we will tell you when it isn’t. Freight in both directions adds real cost and a second set of hands to trust, and for most furniture a good local workshop is the better answer. Shipping earns its keep when a piece is rare or irreplaceable to the family and there is no comparable shop within reach. Send photos; if the honest answer is a restorer closer to home, that is the answer you’ll get.
