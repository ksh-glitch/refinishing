Guide No. 02 · Judgment · From the bench

Is it worth restoring?

It is the question we answer more often than any other, and the only honest reply begins with another question: worth what, to whom? Thirty-five years at the bench have taught us that the answer is rarely about money alone — and that a workshop should have no interest in restoring a piece that shouldn’t be. Here is how we think it through, so you can too.

01 — Two LedgersMarket & memory

What a piece is worth

Two kinds of worth.

Every piece of furniture carries two values: what it would bring at auction, and what it means at the dinner table. Only one of them appears in a price guide.

The market’s ledger is narrow. It rewards rarity, period, maker, and condition — and it changes its mind with fashion. The family’s ledger is wider: the chest that crossed an ocean, the table where every holiday happened, the vanity a grandfather built with his own hands. The market never sees that column at all.

Most of what reaches our bench is here because of the second ledger, and we think that is exactly as it should be. But the two shouldn’t be confused. Restoration is a craft decision and a value decision — so before any work begins, it is worth being honest about which kind of worth you are protecting.

02 — The Finish QuestionRefinish · or conserve

Does refinishing decrease value?

The original finish, honestly.

You may have heard that refinishing an antique destroys its value. Like most rules repeated at estate sales, it is true just often enough to be dangerous.

For a genuinely rare, museum-grade piece — the kind collectors and curators compete for — an original finish is part of the object’s history, and keeping that untouched surface can matter far more than making it shine. If your piece belongs in that company, we will say so plainly, and recommend conservation instead: gentle cleaning, stabilizing loose veneer, feeding and waxing the finish that is already there. The honest answer is sometimes to do less.

Most furniture, though, is not in that company — and much of what looks “original” has already failed, darkened past reading, or been refinished once before. A blackened, water-marked or peeling surface protects nothing and hides everything. For those pieces, careful hand refinishing — never dipping tanks, never acids — doesn’t erase what the maker intended. It recovers it.

† The distinction is decided before any stripper is opened, not after. When in doubt, we photograph, examine, and tell you which case yours is.

03 — Reading a PieceFive judgments

Signs it deserves the bench

How we read a piece.

  1. It is solidly built

    Open a drawer and look at the corners. Dovetails, mortise-and-tenon joints, solid wood with real weight to it — construction like that was meant to be repaired, and it can almost always be brought back, even from damage that looks final. A lowboy once arrived at our shop with a side smashed through and all four legs snapped off. It went home rebuilt.

  2. The surfaces are worth saving

    Fine veneer, inlay, marquetry, carving — these are the piece’s handwriting. Bubbled or missing veneer is not a death sentence: loose sections can be steamed back down and lost ones replaced. And what waits underneath an old finish is often better than anyone remembers, as more than one client has discovered at our bench.

  3. It carries a story

    Family history is a kind of provenance the market cannot price. A piece built by a grandparent, carried through a move, or handed down three generations has already earned its place — the only question left is craft, not worth.

  4. The damage is structural, not fatal

    Broken legs, split panels, failed glue joints, loose casework — dramatic, but repairable in solid wood. What can’t be rebuilt is furniture that was never really built: particleboard, staples and photo-printed grain don’t take a second life the way wood does.

  5. And when to let go

    A late, mass-produced piece with no particular construction and no particular story may simply not repay the work — and if that is our reading, we will tell you rather than take the job. Restoration should answer to what the piece is worth to you. When it doesn’t, the kindest thing we can offer is a straight no.

04 — EvidenceFrom the bench book

Three pieces that answered the question

Junk, until it wasn’t.

The secretary above came in blackened to its ball-and-claw feet — the kind of piece a hurried eye writes off. Under the grime waited figured mahogany finer than anyone alive remembered. A client in Washington sent us photos of a walnut dresser she was ready to throw away; on the bench, its wood and workmanship turned out to be the surprise of the job, and three more pieces followed it to the shop.

And the vanity? A client’s grandfather built it, with a matching dresser, in solid walnut — the symmetrical grain beautifully matched by its builder. The veneer had bubbled in places and gone missing in others. We replaced what was lost, steamed the loose sections back down, and finished the walnut in golden oak. No auction record will ever mention that set. It didn’t need one.

05 — QuestionsAsked at the bench

Answered honestly

What clients ask.

Does refinishing an antique decrease its value?

For most furniture, no. A failed, blackened or water-damaged finish hides the wood and does the piece no favors — careful hand refinishing brings back what the maker intended. The exception is a rare, museum-grade piece with its original finish intact: there, the untouched surface is part of the value, and we will recommend conservation — gentle cleaning, stabilizing, waxing — instead of stripping. If your piece falls into that category, we will tell you so before any work begins.

How do I know if my furniture is worth restoring?

Read how it was built. Solid wood, dovetailed drawers, mortise-and-tenon joints, quality veneer or inlay — construction like that can almost always be brought back, even from serious damage. Then weigh what the piece means to you: a story the market never sees is still value. Send us photos and we will give you an honest reading either way.

When should a piece be conserved instead of refinished?

When an original finish survives on a piece that is rare or historically important, keeping that surface usually matters more than making it shine. Conservation means cleaning, stabilizing loose veneer and feeding the existing finish rather than replacing it. Pieces of that rank are uncommon at the bench, but they exist — and the honest answer is sometimes to do less.

What if my piece only has sentimental value?

Sentimental value is real value — much of the work in our shop is done for it. We have restored a walnut dresser and vanity set handcrafted by a client’s grandfather: no auction record will ever mention it, and it may be the most important commission of that year. If a piece matters to your family, restoring it is worth considering; we will be honest with you about what the work involves so you can decide with clear eyes.
